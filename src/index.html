Photography Write-Off Guide

Deductible expenses vs. non-deductible personal costs based on IRS standards.

What You CAN Deduct

Professional Gear (Canon focus)

Cameras, RF Lenses, CPS Platinum membership, repair fees, lighting, and batteries. (IRS Pub 535)

Subscriptions & Software

Adobe Creative Cloud, CRM (Honeybook/Dubsado), Gallery hosting (Pixieset/Pic-Time).

Business Phone & Internet

100% of the phone bill if on an LLC Business Plan. Proportionate % of home internet for editing. (IRS Pub 463)

Marketing & SEO

Google Ads, Social Media advertising, website hosting, and business cards.

Professional Services

CPA fees, legal templates for contracts, and PPA membership.

IRS Publication 535: Business Expenses →

What You CANNOT Deduct

Commuting Miles

Driving from home to a "regular place of business" (like a fixed studio) is not deductible. Driving home to a wedding venue is.

Personal Clothing

You cannot deduct "wedding attire" or nice clothes just because you wear them to shoots. Only uniforms or safety gear qualify.

Personal Grooming

Haircuts, makeup for yourself, or dry cleaning for personal clothes are strictly non-deductible.

Entertainment

Under current tax laws, you generally cannot deduct "entertainment" like taking a client to a sports game or a movie. (IRS Pub 463)

Family Meals

Meals with family members are not deductible unless they are employees and you are discussing legitimate business during travel.

IRS Publication 463: Travel & Meals →

Write-Off Best Practices (Audit Proofing)

  • The "Receipt Rule"

    IRS requires records for any expense >$75. Digital copies are valid. Pro tip: Photograph your receipts immediately.

  • Business Meals (50%)

    Deductible only if you, the owner, are present and business is discussed. (Current rule: 50% limit).

  • Mileage Tracking (Essential)

    Use MileIQ or Stride. Arizona photographers often find mileage is their largest "silent" deduction ($0.67/mile in 2024).

  • The Home Office (Pub 587)

    Space must be used *exclusively* for business. "Mixed use" rooms are a red flag for the IRS.
